Is There a Pattern to the Upset?

March Madness  ·  2008–2025  ·  NCAA Tournament

Every March, millions of brackets are filled out — and promptly destroyed. We tend to call upsets chaos, but what if they aren't random at all? Across 17 years of NCAA Tournament data, a clear pattern emerges: certain eras are more upset-prone, certain seed matchups produce chaos almost on cue, and certain underdogs punch above their weight year after year. The madness has a method.

Round 2008 – 2025

Upset frequency may not be random, it may move in eras

If upsets were pure chance, we'd expect the count to hover around the same number every year, but they don't seem to do that. The data breaks into three distinct eras. The pre-2016 period was relatively stable with 8 to 11 upsets most years. Then came a stretch of high-chaos tournaments peaking around 2022–2023, when 14 or more upsets became the norm. The trend line makes this drift visible.

Two external forces help explain the shift. The 2021 tournament that was played in a COVID bubble with no home crowds produced a notably low upset count, likely because home-court momentum was stripped away. Then starting in 2022, the NCAA's NIL rules gave players new financial freedom to transfer freely. Mid-major programs could now recruit and retain talent that previously flowed only to powerhouses and the upset numbers reflect it.

Some seed matchups are reliably dangerous

If upsets were random, every cell in this grid would be roughly the same shade. The 12-over-5 and 11-over-6 matchups appear so frequently they're practically expected. Savvy bracket-fillers have known this for years. But the pattern runs deeper than just those famous matchups. Certain diagonal bands light up consistently, suggesting the gap between a 4-seed and a 5-seed matters far less than the gap between a 1-seed and a 2-seed.

Notice what's mostly dark: upsets involving 1-seeds are rare enough to be memorable precisely because they break this pattern. Certain seeds are dangerous by design, not by accident

Win rate alone doesn't tell the full story. A 5-seed has an easier path than an 11-seed, so raw winning percentages aren't directly comparable. The PAKE score (Performance Above KenPom Expectation) controls for strength of schedule: a positive value means a seed wins more often than their talent level predicts. That's the mark of a structural underdog advantage, not a fluke.

Seeds 11 and 12 post the highest PAKE scores in the dataset. The leading theory for this is that these teams are typically strong mid-major programs or Power-5 bubble teams that just squeaked into the field. They're battle-tested, overlooked, and playing with nothing to lose against a 6 or 5 seed that may have coasted through a weaker conference. That combination - competitive team, low expectations, fired-up opponent — is a repeatable recipe for chaos.

Upsets aren't decided by miracle margins

One common assumption is that upsets are close games won on a last-second shot, barely surviving. The data mostly disagrees. Upset margins are slightly narrower than expected wins on average, but the difference is smaller than you'd think, particularly in the Round of 64. Many upsets are decided by 8–12 points, which looks less like a miracle and more like a team that simply matched up well.

One important caveat is this chart can overstate how dominant upsets look. A Cinderella team that wins in the first round and goes on a deep run contributes multiple games to the upset column, so sustained runs by a single team skew the data. The pattern still holds directionally, but treat the exact margins with some skepticism. So is there a pattern?

Yes. The data makes a clear case that NCAA Tournament upsets are not random noise. They cluster by era, by seed matchup, and by team archetype. The 12-over-5 isn't a surprise anymore, it's an expectation. Seeds 11 and 12 don't just get lucky. They structurally outperform their talent level, tournament after tournament.

What changed post-2021 seems to be very real. The transfer portal and NIL have flattened the talent gap between mid-major and Power-5 programs, and the upset numbers reflect it. The madness has a method, you just have to know where to look.
